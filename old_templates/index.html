Medical Malpractice Trends in the US:

Over the last 3 decades, the number and cost of medical malpractice cases have been steadily increasing. The financial burden of handling this problem often falls on practitioners and medical institutions with occasional support from various state funds. Hence medical malpractice cases contribute to the increasing cost of healthcare in the US. This issue further necessitates the use of state and federal juridical resources. In order to alleviate this burden various policies have been implemented at the state level with varying degrees of success. In this project we attempt to provide an analysis of such policies and management approaches and make recommendations to states and institutions to aid them in lowering the risk and cost of malpractice cases.

Here, we provide an analysis of the data set collected and provided by the National Practitioner Data Bank (NPDB) on malpractice cases over the span of the last 25 years which includes information on things such as the license field of the practitioner, age and gender of the patient, geography, and final costs among other relevant details for a large collection of medical malpractice records. Here, you can see the fields of license which are most likely to result in malpractice cases. In order to get a sense of which states have been performing well in terms of lowering the likelihood of malpractice cases, a linear regression analysis was performed for each state which were then clustered according to their rates of decrease in malpractice cases per capita.

For example, Michigan presents with the highest rate of success, while Washington has had major increases in its malpractice case rates over the last 25 years.
